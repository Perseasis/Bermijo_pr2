Paula Scher’s career is a story of innovation, boldness, and a commitment to pushing the boundaries of graphic design. She has worked across a variety of disciplines, from branding and advertising to environmental design and art direction, and has become one of the most influential figures in contemporary graphic design.

Scher’s career began in the late 1960s and early 1970s. She started working in the design industry in New York, initially as a designer for CBS Records, where she was responsible for album covers. This early exposure to visual storytelling through music packaging gave her the opportunity to experiment with vibrant, bold designs, often working with typography in innovative ways. Some of her notable work during this period includes the album covers for artists like The Rolling Stones and Bob Dylan, which displayed her characteristic flair for dramatic typography and visual impact. In the 1970s, Scher moved to the prestigious design agency Pentagram (in 1991, she became the first female partner there), where she made her mark as a creative director and designer. At Pentagram, Scher would evolve her approach to graphic design, focusing not just on how to create beautiful visual designs but also on how to communicate ideas and identities in bold, powerful ways.

Scher has received numerous awards and accolades for her groundbreaking work in graphic design. She was inducted into the Art Directors Club Hall of Fame and has received multiple Grammy Awards for her album cover designs. Her work has been exhibited in leading galleries and museums around the world, and she has been the subject of retrospectives at major institutions like the Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum.
Her career is also defined by a fearless approach to design. Scher is not afraid to push boundaries, challenge conventions, and explore new ways of communicating visually. Whether designing for a corporate client or a cultural institution, she brings a unique, innovative perspective to every project. Today, Paula Scher is considered one of the most important figures in contemporary graphic design. Her influence extends far beyond her own work, having shaped the way designers think about typography, branding, and visual communication. She continues to inspire design
